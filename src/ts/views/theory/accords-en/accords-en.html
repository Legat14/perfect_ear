Chords

A chord (from the Latin akordare - to agree) is an independent consonance, built according to a certain principle. Note that we can also call an interval (two sounds) a consonance, but in a chord, as a rule, there are three or more sounds.

Traditional music theory separates the concepts of chord and non-chord (the construction of a random combination of several sounds).

Until the 20th century, only such independent consonances were considered chords , the sounds of which can be arranged in thirds ( terts chords ).

Note: Third is an interval of three steps. A minor third (m.3/1.5 tones) contains one and a half tones, a major (b.3/2 tones), a diminished third (min.3/1 tone) and an augmented third (increased 3/2.5 tones). The correct construction of thirds will help in mastering chords.

If you build thirds on the piano, then on the white keys (on the main steps), three large thirds are built - from do, fa and sol, and four small thirds - from re, mi, la and si. Knowing that the sol-si is a major third, and seeing accidentals near the notes, you can guess about its changes. If there are flats or sharps near the notes of salt and si, then the third will remain large (sol-flat - si-flat, sol-sharp - si-sharp). A sharp near the note sol or flat near si will make the third small (sol-sharp - si, sol - si-flat).

Triad

Triad - consonance, chord, the sounds of which are arranged in thirds (extreme sounds form a fifth).

There are four types of triads and differ in their interval composition : major (large), minor (small), diminished, enlarged.

The sound from which the chord is arranged in thirds is called the root tone. Next comes the third (second sound) and fifth tone (third sound). If the root is in the bass of the chord, then it is the root form of the chord . If the bass has a different chord tone, then this is an inversion (for more details, see the inversion of chords article).

Triads in major and minor

The major and minor modes are determined by the main steps - I (tonic / T ), IV (subdominant / S ) and V (dominant / D ).

It is on these steps in major that major triads and minor triads in minor are built. They are about again any harmony in major or minor compositions and are called major triads . The remaining chords are secondary and carry an additional function.

Three major triads (I, IV, V), three minor triads (II, III, VI) and one diminished triad (VII) can be built on the steps of the major scale. If you use the harmonic form of major , in which the sixth step is lowered by half a step, then you can also build a reduced and minor triad, as well as an increased triad.

On the steps of the minor scale , you can build three minor triads (I, IV, V), three major triads (III, VI, VII) and one diminished triad (II). If you use the harmonic form of the minor , in which the seventh degree rises by half a step, then you can build an even reduced and major triad, as well as an increased triad. Note that in minor compositions , the harmonic type is most often used, so the triad on the fifth step (dominant) will always be major.

The sound of a non-chord determines the interval composition. A fifth tone may be omitted in a non-chord.

Triad inversions

The triad has two inversions: the sixth chord (inv.1) and the fourth-sextchord (inv.2).

Sextachord ( inv.1 )

If the lower sound of the triad is moved an octave higher, then the first type of treatment will be obtained - a sixth chord, in the bass the third tone of the chord.

Its structure is third + fourth (3 + 4).
