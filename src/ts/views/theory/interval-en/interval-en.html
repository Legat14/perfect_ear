Intervals

Intervals are a fundamental unit in music and an integral part of melody and chord.According to Boris Asafiev, "each interval is achieved (recognized) by ear, and the melody, in essence, is the identification of intervals"

An interval (from the Latin intervallum - interval, distance) is the ratio of two sounds in height.

Two interval sounds can sound simultaneously or sequentially , in which case the interval will be melodic (successively) or harmonic (simultaneously).

The interval has a base - a lower sound, and a top - an upper sound.

For example, an ascending melodic do-mi interval moves from top to bottom.

The melodic interval can be ascending (from the base to the top) or descending (from the top to the base).

Harmonic intervals are always read from bottom to top.

Interval classification

Intervals are classified by quantitative (step) and qualitative (tone) value.

Quantitative (step) value - the number of main steps of the scale (do-re-mi-fa-sol-la-si) from the base to the top inclusive.

The quantitative value determines the name of the interval, which is a Latin ordinal number corresponding to the number of steps in the interval.

For example, the number of steps in the interval in Fig.3 and Fig.7 is equal to four (do-re-mi-fa from bottom to top) - this interval will be called a quart (from Latin quartus - fourth), and in Fig.1, 2, 5, 6 - three (do-re-mi and do-re-mi-flat) - this is a third (from Latin tertius - third).

Intervals within an octave inclusive are called simple , and wider than an octave - compound .

For example, in Fig.1-8 - simple intervals, and in Fig.1-8.9 - composite interval.

Fig.3 Harmonic composite interval re-fa - decima (third through an octave).

Qualitative (tone) value - the number of semitones (or tones) between the base and top of the interval.

Note: A semitone is the smallest distance between two nearest tones of different pitches.

Fig.10 Rising melodic second G-A-flat (semitone).

For example, the number of semitones in the interval in Fig.3, 7 (quart) is equal to five semitones (or two and a half tones), and in Fig.1, 5 (major third) - four semitones (or two tones), in Fig.2, 6 (small third) – three semitones (one and a half tones), in Fig.4, 8 (pure fifth) - seven semitones (three and a half tones).

The qualitative value determines the quality (type) of the interval and divides the intervals into pure, small, large, reduced, increased.

For example, in Fig.3, 7 - pure quart, in Fig.2, 6 – minor third, in Fig.1, 5 major third, in Fig.4, 8 - pure fifth.

Prims, fourths, fifths and octaves are pure intervals.If they sound in a harmonic form, then they can be determined by the nature of the sound (phonism) - these are perfect consonances .

Seconds, thirds, sixths and sevenths are large and small, depending on the qualitative (tone) value.Harmonic seconds and sevenths are dissonances , and thirds and sixths are imperfect consonances .

Any interval can become decreased (except unison) and increased.As part of our exercises, we will use only the diminished fifth and duodecime, as well as the augmented fourth and undecima.Augmented fourth and diminished fifth are also called tritones (because they contain three tones).

Note: Consonance (from the French consonance - consonant sound).Consonant consonance is psychologically perceived as harmonious, soft, calm.There are perfect and imperfect consonances.

Dissonance (from the French dissonance - discordant sound).Dissonance is psychologically perceived by the ear as disharmonious, tense, restless.
