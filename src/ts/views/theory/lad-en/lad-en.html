Modes

First, let's deal with the concept of " mode " (from the Latin modus - measure, method).

Mode and interval are two interrelated and most important categories of musical thinking, where mode is a system of a certain logical organization of tones and semitones, and interval is the smallest intonation cell of a mode. For the formation of a musical ear, the importance of the mode, as the basis of musical logic, is undeniable.

So, mode is a limited pitch system, with a certain interval structure, on the basis of which music is created. Each mode sound is called a step. The steps of the mode are divided into stable and unstable.

For example , the scale of the Ionian and major modes: tone - tone - semitone - tone - tone - tone - semitone (we talked about tones and semitones in the "Intervals" section).

For example , the first step, as well as the third and fifth, are considered stable steps. The steps of the mode, in turn, are traditionally indicated by Roman numerals I - III - V (stable steps of the mode), II - IV - VI - VII (unstable steps of the mode).

Note: The stepwise ascending or descending melodic movement of the scale of the fret within at least an octave is called gamma .

In professional European music, a system of seven-step frets has developed. Most likely this is due to the optimal amount of human RAM. However, in folklore music there are dichords (a mode of two steps), trichords (of three steps), tetrachords (of four steps), pentachords or pentatonics (of five steps), etc. Both the scales of the modes and the rules for the behavior of their steps have historically developed in musical practice.

For the expressiveness of the frets, the value of the third from the first step is of great importance. Therefore, there are, for example, major and minor modes, pentatonic major or minor mood and color.

Note: Major (from French majeur and Italian maggiore - larger, also dur from Latin durus - hard) is a natural scale, the scale of which in the ascending movement has the structure tone - tone - semitone - tone - tone - tone - semitone.

Minor (from Italian minore - smaller, from Latin moll - soft) is a natural scale, the scale of which in the ascending movement has the structure tone - semitone - tone - tone - semitone - tone - tone.

As we know, the distance between the white keys is different, so each note will build a fret with a special tonal structure, so we get seven different frets: Dorian, Phrygian, Lydian, Mixolydian, Ionian, Aeolian, Locrian (the names are borrowed from ancient Greek music). ).

Any fret, knowing its tonal structure, can be built from any note.

For example, to build the Mixolydian mode from the note E, you need to build a major mode with a lowered seventh degree. And to build a Phrygian mode from note to, you need to build a minor mode with a lowered second degree.

The historical evolution of modal systems from the era of classicism to the 20th century went along the line of enriching the sound composition of the mode. In this regard, the tasks of developing ear in motion from the classical major-minor through a single major-minor system of the same name, seven-step diatonic modes and modal variability (combination of major and minor) to complex modal structures in the music of the 20th century become more complicated. For example, “symmetrical modes” are a whole-tone scale, tone-semitone scale or semitone-tone etc.

On the basis of the mode, not only melody is built, but also harmony. Moreover, harmony and harmony have a significant impact on each other. For example, mode changes the interval structure of chords.

In the Renaissance, a mode with such a scale was called the Ionian. He had two basic steps - the first and fifth, they were more often than others in the melody. The rest of the steps are inert. After any stage, any stage can appear. In the 17th century, the steps of the same scale were divided into three stable and four unstable ones. The new mode was called the Major. Melodies and polyphony in the Ionic and major modes differ markedly in expressiveness.
